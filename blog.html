How to Build a Faraday Cage for Your Mobile Devices Using Household Items to Block IMSI-Catchers

If you are reading this, you already know that IMSI-catchers — colloquially known as Stingrays — are mobile surveillance devices used by law enforcement agencies and, increasingly, unknown private actors to intercept cell communications, track physical locations, and force-downgrade phones from 4G/5G to older, more vulnerable 2G protocols. This tutorial is purely theoretical and educational. Building a Faraday cage is legal and has many legitimate applications.

What is an IMSI-Catcher?

An IMSI (International Mobile Subscriber Identity) catcher impersonates a legitimate cell tower. Your device, always hunting for the strongest signal, connects to the fake tower instead of the real one. The device captures your IMSI number — a unique identifier that persists across SIM swaps. From your IMSI, a carrier or court can identify you. Some models (AMSII, Gemini II) can actively intercept voice calls and SMS in real time.

Detection is difficult because IMSI catchers operate passively. Countermeasures include:

  • Using a VoIP-over-Tor setup instead of cellular for voice calls
  • Disabling 2G fallback in developer options (Android: Settings → Network → Preferred network type → LTE only)
  • Using IMSI catcher detector apps such as AIMSICD or SnoopSnitch
  • Physically blocking RF transmission — the Faraday cage approach

Materials Required

  • Aluminium foil (kitchen grade — 3+ layers work well)
  • A metal tin or biscuit box with a tight-fitting lid
  • Aluminium mesh (window screen material from a hardware store)
  • Conductive copper tape (electrician's supply, ~$6 per roll)
  • Multimeter (optional, for testing attenuation)

Method 1 — The Aluminium Foil Wrap

The simplest approach. Wrap your device in 4–6 layers of aluminium foil, ensuring no gaps. A single layer provides approximately 30–50 dB of RF attenuation in the 700–2100 MHz range used by LTE. Six layers approaches 90–100 dB — effectively a blackout. Test by calling the wrapped device. No ring = success.

Weakness: It must be unwrapped every time you use the device. Not practical for daily use. Best for when you need to carry a device without broadcasting your location.

Method 2 — The Tin Box Faraday Cage

A metal tin (cookie tin, ammunition box, or military surplus "dry box") provides a reusable and robust Faraday cage. The critical requirement is a conductive seal where the lid meets the body. Most commercial tins have a lip-and-groove fit that is sufficient. Test with a phone: place inside, call it. Silence = good seal.

Improving the Seal

Apply conductive copper tape around the lid-body junction on the inside. This eliminates any gaps in the electromagnetic seal. The frequency we're shielding against (700 MHz–2.6 GHz) requires gaps smaller than ~1 cm to be meaningfully attenuated.

Method 3 — Aluminium Mesh Pouch

Cut two rectangles of aluminium window screen mesh. Fold and crimp the edges together using conductive tape to form a pouch. This creates a lightweight, portable option. The mesh hole size (~1 mm) is much smaller than the wavelengths of cellular and WiFi frequencies, making it highly effective. You can use it like a wallet for your phone.

Testing Your Cage

Test 1 — Voice Call Test:
  1. Call your device from another phone
  2. Place device inside cage / wrap
  3. Seal completely — no gaps
  4. If the phone rings → FAIL (gap exists)
  5. If call goes to voicemail → PASS (cage is blocking RF)

Test 2 — WiFi Test (if you have a WiFi analyzer app):
  1. Note your device's WiFi signal on analyzer
  2. Seal device inside cage
  3. Device should disappear from the WiFi network list → PASS

Test 3 — GPS Test:
  1. Open Google Maps / GPS app outside
  2. Get a GPS fix
  3. Seal device inside cage
  4. GPS fix should be lost within 60 seconds → PASS

Important Notes & Limitations

  • A Faraday cage blocks ALL wireless signals — WiFi, Bluetooth, GPS, NFC, and cellular. The device cannot make or receive calls while shielded.
  • Modern high-power Stingray variants may briefly capture your IMSI before you can seal your device. Ideally, power off the device first, then seal it.
  • This is a passive defensive measure, not an offensive one. Using RF jamming devices is illegal in most jurisdictions.
  • The best IMSI catcher countermeasure is not carrying a phone at all during sensitive activities.
